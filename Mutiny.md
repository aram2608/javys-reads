Chapter 10 the campus of Dow chemical

The united auto workers bargaining events were almost ritualistic in nature. The president would photo op with the ceo of a company, ford, General Motors, Chrysler, then pick a company to negotiate with. Once a deal was met, the other companies would sign similar deals.

When a deal wasn’t met, strikes would only occur at the company where negations were occurring. This left the other two largely unscathed until everything was resolved

Union leaders and CEOs guarded the proposals and each sides points with an obsessive jealousy, ostensibly so that the negotiations were fair, however, most agreed it shielded companies from public shaming by union members and protected union leaders from getting chastised for lousy deals. This whole deal crumbled in 2023

Shawn Fain, the newest president at UAW, made some rather generous demands and completely disregarded mos maorum. He publicly bashed the companies’ proposals and inflamed his workers, calling a strike on one of each of the companies factories, threatening to expand.

The UAW expanded to graduate students around the 1980s, recognizing auto off shoring may kill the union. Graduate workers ironically resemble factory workers, taking on massive workloads for minimum pay with marginal benefits all to pump out bachelors students from the classes they teach. The cogs of a fucked up scam to get grads in and out the door with precision efficiency 

UAW was run through the Administrative Caucus, basically a political party for the union, that was entirely undemocratic and punished reformists severely. However, the influx of left leaning graduate students in the union as well as other prominent reformers lobbied incredibly hard and got some change.

25 years ago graduate students pushed to get DOW Chemical off campus, given its selling of Agent Orange and implications in the Vietnam war. Now, the campuses are Dow Chemical, looking to minimize costs and maximize profits, like a corporation. They also argued that universities pushed for as many grad students as possible, creating a glut of talent that pits students against each other, depressing wages, and decreasing prospects, shifting power into the hands of the employers. A classic example of too many people racing for too few jobs. In economics, too many dollars chasing too few goods causes inflation, too few dollars chasing too many goods causing deflation. The principle applies similarly

Graduates are increasingly shifting left with more radical opinions
